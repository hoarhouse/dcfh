The Vatican’s AI Revolution: How Rome Got Big Tech to Sign a Moral Code

In 2020, tech giants met the Vatican to sign the Rome Call for AI Ethics. Five years later, its six moral principles still shape global debates on how humanity should guide artificial intelligence.

It sounds like the setup for a satire. A handful of the most powerful tech executives on Earth—Microsoft, IBM, Cisco—fly to Rome to meet priests and philosophers. The setting: the Vatican's marble-lined Casina Pio IV, a Renaissance villa usually reserved for papal science councils. The mission: to sign a document about artificial intelligence.

Five years later, in 2025, that day feels less quaint than prophetic. The event became known as The Rome Call for AI Ethics—the moment faith met code, and moral philosophy walked straight into Silicon Valley's backyard.

A Different Kind of Summit

On February 28, 2020, just weeks before the world shut down for the pandemic, something unprecedented happened inside Vatican City. Executives from Microsoft and IBM sat beside cardinals, rabbis, and U.N. officials, pen in hand, to endorse six ethical principles for AI.

No venture capital, no product demo—just a shared statement on how machines should treat human beings.

For a tech culture used to "move fast and break things," Rome offered the opposite rhythm: think slowly and build wisely. The Vatican wasn't promising funding or regulation. What it offered was older, rarer, and harder to ignore—2,000 years of moral infrastructure and a global audience that spans every border and faith.

"We needed a convening power that wasn't beholden to any government or corporation," one participant later recalled. "The Vatican was the only place that could put everyone at the same table and make them listen."

Why the Vatican Mattered

Pope Francis had been talking about AI long before ChatGPT turned it into dinner-table conversation. He worried not about robots taking jobs, but about algorithms taking judgment—systems deciding who gets a loan, a job, or parole without any real understanding of human dignity.

In the months before the summit, he warned that technology "should never determine the fate of individuals and societies without moral reflection." But he didn't just want to scold. He wanted to convene.

So his science advisors—among them Fr. Paolo Benanti, a Franciscan friar trained in engineering and bioethics—drafted a plan: gather the world's AI powerbrokers and challenge them to articulate shared principles before the technology hardened into global infrastructure.

The Six Principles That Emerged

Out of that improbable collaboration came six deceptively simple words—transparency, inclusion, responsibility, impartiality, reliability, and security. Together they became the Rome Call for AI Ethics, a kind of Ten Commandments for code.

  1. Transparency – People should know when and how an algorithm makes a decision about them.
  2. Inclusion – AI must serve everyone, not just those designing or affording it.
  3. Responsibility – Humans stay accountable; machines cannot carry moral blame.
  4. Impartiality – Systems must detect and reduce bias rather than amplify it.
  5. Reliability – AI should do what it promises, safely and predictably.
  6. Security and Privacy – Data belongs first to the individual, not the institution.

No legal teeth. No penalties. But the signatures—Brad Smith of Microsoft, John Kelly III of IBM, senior Vatican officials, rabbis, imams, and even representatives of the Italian government—gave it weight.

When Smith later described the event as "a turning point in how we think about technology's role in society," he wasn't exaggerating. For the first time, the moral language of should entered a conversation dominated by can.

The Moment It Landed

The timing was uncanny. By early 2020, headlines about biased facial-recognition systems, discriminatory hiring algorithms, and extremist-fueling social feeds were everywhere. Governments were starting to regulate; consumers were losing trust.

The tech industry needed legitimacy. The Vatican needed a new way to apply its social teaching to modern life. The Rome Call gave both sides what they wanted—a moral handshake that made ethics feel like innovation rather than obstruction.

Behind the optics, though, something deeper stirred. Francis had reframed AI not as a technical challenge but as a human-rights issue. The question wasn't how to perfect the code, but how to preserve conscience in a data-driven world.

After the Flashbulbs

Then the world changed. COVID-19 emptied airports and silenced conferences. But the Rome Call kept echoing.

Within a year, the European Union cited several of its principles in drafting the AI Act—especially requirements for transparency and human oversight. The Vatican had no seat in Brussels, yet its phrasing slipped into policy memos and academic syllabi.

Tech giants, too, began referencing the Call in their internal frameworks. Microsoft's Responsible AI guidelines echo its six principles almost word-for-word. IBM used the document in employee ethics training. Whether that reflected conviction or clever branding is debatable—but at least it gave journalists and watchdogs a moral ruler to measure against.

Universities adopted it; interfaith coalitions expanded it. By 2023, Buddhist, Hindu, and Indigenous leaders had joined follow-up meetings, turning the original trio of faiths into a global dialogue.

What began as a photo op became a vocabulary.

The Critics, and the Caveats

Not everyone bought the Vatican's miracle narrative. Scholars pointed out that "human dignity" and "common good," while noble, are vague in code audits or compliance checklists. Others noted that the Rome Call didn't address the real engines of AI harm—data monopolies, economic incentives, and concentration of power.

And yes, it lacked enforcement. Companies could sign on Friday and violate the principles by Monday.

Fr. Benanti, who helped draft the document, was candid:

"We didn't solve AI ethics in a day. We began a conversation. The question is whether that conversation changes behavior—or just provides cover for business as usual."

Still, critics admit that moral frameworks rarely start perfect. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights wasn't enforceable either; its power came from consensus, not coercion.

Why It Still Matters

For the Vatican, the Call was never about compliance—it was about conscience. By drawing tech leaders into moral language, it forced a culture defined by disruption to pause and ask why.

That, arguably, is Rome's superpower. It can convene where others compete. It can talk about centuries in an industry obsessed with quarters. As Cardinal Peter Turkson said at the signing:

"Tech companies think in quarters. Governments think in election cycles. The Church thinks in centuries."

The genius of the Rome Call wasn't theological; it was temporal. It widened the horizon.

A Broader Mission

Since 2020, the Vatican's AI efforts have expanded. The Pontifical Academy for Life now hosts yearly "Rome Call" follow-ups on topics like:

  • AI and climate modeling
  • Algorithmic warfare
  • Automation and inequality
  • Generative AI and authorship
  • End-of-life care technologies

Instead of just executives and clerics, the guest list now includes artists, ethicists, and activists. The idea: keep the circle widening until AI ethics becomes a conversation, not a code.

In parallel, Francis has woven the same themes into his encyclicals—urging that technology serve the integral development of the person, not just economic growth. He frames it not as a ban, but as a balance: innovation with inclusion, progress with purpose.

Lessons for the Rest of Us

Even outside faith circles, the Rome Call offers durable takeaways:

  • Ethics needs diversity. Rules made by coders alone won't reflect the world they shape.
  • Principles must become practice. Signing a charter is easy; redesigning incentives is not.
  • Time horizons matter. Ethical failure is often the child of short-term thinking.
  • Some questions aren't technical. "Should we?" is a moral verb, not a computational one.

In a sense, the Vatican's AI revolution is less about religion than about reminding the world that moral imagination still counts as a kind of intelligence.

Looking Back from 2025

Five years on, no one pretends the Rome Call fixed AI. Bias still infects datasets. Automation still displaces workers. Surveillance still erodes privacy.

But the Call did something subtle and enduring: it changed the tone. It made moral language acceptable again in tech circles that once dismissed it as medieval.

When the next generation of policymakers writes AI law, when engineers debate algorithmic fairness, they now borrow phrases minted in Rome: transparency, inclusion, accountability.

And maybe that's how ethical revolutions actually begin—not with commandments carved in stone, but with a conversation that refuses to end.
