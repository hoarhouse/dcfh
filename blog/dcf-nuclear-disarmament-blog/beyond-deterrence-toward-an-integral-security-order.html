    The year is 2026. The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) Review Conference, held in April and May, has just ended in failure. What should have been a forum for cooperation collapsed into harsh rhetoric, mutual accusations, and the chilling reappearance of nuclear threats as instruments of political bargaining. Escalation looms, and the world seems closer to legitimizing nuclear violence than at any point in recent memory.

    On 31 May, the Feast of the Visitation of the Blessed Virgin Mary, Pope Leo XIV addresses the crisis. Drawing on the Gospel of Luke, he contrasts the example of Mary visiting Elizabeth ‚Äì an act of peace, humility, and encounter ‚Äì with a world on the verge of nuclear catastrophe. ‚ÄúInstead of carrying life to one another,‚Äù he warns, ‚Äúwe prepare the instruments of death.‚Äù With vigor, he reaffirms the teaching first declared by Pope Francis in 2017: not only the use or threat of nuclear weapons, but even their possession, is morally unacceptable.

    For leaders of nuclear-armed states ‚Äì particularly those where Christian tradition still resonates ‚Äì this declaration triggers alarm. Defense ministries scramble to assess the implications for military doctrine. Political elites recognize that the moral foundations of deterrence, long questioned but never fully displaced, are now under renewed and urgent scrutiny. Within days, high-level negotiations are convened, exploring what a nuclear order rooted not in fear but in integral security could look like.

    This is not an idle scenario. In 2017, when Francis made his historic statement in Rome, survivors of Hiroshima and Nagasaki ‚Äì the hibakusha ‚Äì stood before the world as living testimony[i]. Their words cut through the abstractions of strategy and deterrence, reminding policymakers that nuclear weapons erase families, cultures, and futures. That moral shock created space for dialogue, drawing even adversaries into conversation under the Vatican roof.

    Integral security builds on that legacy. Its central claim is that security is indivisible: nuclear stability cannot be isolated from human dignity, diplomacy, or legitimacy. A doctrine that sustains political control but corrodes moral trust cannot endure. True stability requires weaving together arms control, humanitarian principles, and ethical legitimacy into a coherent framework.

    The task is urgent. Nuclear deterrence, forged in the Cold War‚Äôs bipolar logic, is breaking down in a multipolar age marked by unpredictable rivalries and accelerating technologies. Risks of miscalculation rise, while the destructive potential remains absolute. Integral security points to a different horizon: negotiations that do not simply trade warheads, but integrate military restraint with human-centered principles of peace, justice, and survival.

    Here, the defense industry itself has a role. Companies that once designed weapons of mass destruction can become partners in reshaping security around life rather than annihilation. Investing in technologies that strengthen verification, secure command systems, and foster stability can align industry not against peace, but with it. The choice is stark: remain bound to a doctrine that history and morality have condemned, or help design a future where security and human dignity stand together.

    Alongside helping to shape the internal drafting of the Pope‚Äôs historic speech on the immorality of nuclear weapons, I oversaw the Holy See‚Äôs wider role in arms control and disarmament ‚Äì and if faith could open paths to dialogue where politics had failed, then it is a call to all my peers, and to all men and women of good will, to take up the same responsibility in our digital age: to reshape security together with courage, ethics, and hope.[ii]

    As Pope Leo XIV reminds us, the Visitation is an icon of encounter: life approaching life, hope meeting hope. In a world shadowed by nuclear fire, that same spirit must guide us now. The time has come to move beyond deterrence, and toward an order of integral security.
